**Yes, it is possible for transition T5 to fire before transition T3.**  

**Concrete firing sequence:**  
Initial marking: \( p0 = 1 \)  
1. Fire **T1**: consumes token from \( p0 \), produces one token in \( p1 \) and one in \( p2 \).  
   Marking: \( p1 = 1, p2 = 1 \)  
2. Fire **T2**: consumes token from \( p1 \), produces one token in \( p3 \).  
   Marking: \( p2 = 1, p3 = 1 \)  
3. Fire **T5**: enabled because \( p3 \) has a token and \( p4 \) has **zero** tokens (inhibitor arc satisfied).  
   Consumes token from \( p3 \), produces one token in \( p6 \).  
   Marking: \( p2 = 1, p6 = 1 \)  

At this point, T5 has fired while T3 has **not** fired yet (the token in \( p2 \) remains).  

**Justification:**  
- After T1, both T2 and T3 are enabled, but we choose to fire T2 first.  
- After T2, \( p3 \) gets a token and \( p4 \) is still empty (since T3 hasn’t fired).  
- T5’s inhibitor arc condition (\( p4 = 0 \)) is satisfied, so T5 can fire immediately.  
- T3 remains enabled throughout (since \( p2 \) still has its token), but we simply fire T5 before T3.  

Thus, the sequence **T1 → T2 → T5** shows T5 firing before T3 is possible.
