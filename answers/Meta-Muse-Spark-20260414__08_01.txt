**Yes — T5 can fire before T3.**

### Concrete firing sequence from the initial marking
Initial marking: `M0 = [p0=1, p1=0, p2=0, p3=0, p4=0, p5=0, p6=0]`

1. **Fire T1**: `p0 → p1 + p2`  
   `M1 = [p0=0, p1=1, p2=1, p3=0, p4=0, p5=0, p6=0]`

2. **Fire T2**: `p1 → p3`  
   `M2 = [p0=0, p1=0, p2=1, p3=1, p4=0, p5=0, p6=0]`

3. **Fire T5**: needs `p3 ≥ 1` and `p4 = 0` due to inhibitor arc `–|p4`  
   Both conditions hold in `M2`, so T5 can fire: `p3 → p6`  
   `M3 = [p0=0, p1=0, p2=1, p3=0, p4=0, p5=0, p6=1]`

T3 never fired in that sequence, so T5 fired before T3.

### Why this works
After the parallel split T1, the net has tokens in both `p1` and `p2`. 

- `p1` enables T2, which puts a token in `p3`.
- As long as `p2` hasn’t been consumed by T3 yet, `p4` remains at 0.

So once we get `p3=1, p4=0`, T5’s enabling condition `p3 ∧ (p4=0)` is satisfied. T3 is also enabled at that moment because `p2=1`, but there’s no requirement that T3 must fire first. The inhibitor on T5 only blocks it *after* T3 puts a token in `p4`. 

Therefore T5 can fire before T3, and doing so permanently disables T4 because T4 needs both `p3` and `p4`, and T5 consumes `p3` while `p4` is still empty.
