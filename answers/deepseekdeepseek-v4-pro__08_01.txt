**Yes**, it is possible for T5 to fire before T3.

**Concrete firing sequence from the initial marking:**
1. **Fire T1**: consumes token from p0, produces tokens in p1 and p2.  
   Marking: p1=1, p2=1, all others empty.
2. **Fire T2**: consumes token from p1, produces token in p3.  
   Marking: p2=1, p3=1, p4=0.
3. **Fire T5**: consumes token from p3, requires p4=0 (true), produces token in p6.  
   Marking: p2=1, p6=1.

T3 remains unfired during this entire sequence. It is still enabled after step 3 (since p2=1), proving that T5 fired strictly *before* T3.

**Justification:** The parallel split after T1 enables independent execution of the two branches (p1 → T2 → p3 and p2 → T3 → p4). By firing T2 first, we produce a token in p3 while p4 is still empty. This satisfies the inhibitor condition of T5 (p4=0) and its input requirement (p3≥1), making T5 immediately enabled and allowing it to fire before the other branch advances via T3.
