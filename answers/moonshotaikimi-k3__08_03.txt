**Yes.** The shortcut transition **T2b** puts a token directly into `pd` without ever producing `pc`, so the join **T5** can be satisfied while **T3** has not fired (indeed `pc` stays 0, so T3 is not even enabled beforehand).

**One shortest firing sequence (length 4):**

\[
\sigma = T1,\ T2b,\ T4,\ T5
\]

(The middle two are independent, so `T1, T4, T2b, T5` is equally shortest.)

Marking trace `(p0,pa,pb,pc,pd,pe,pf)`:

| step | marking | note |
|---|---|---|
| initial | (1,0,0,0,0,0,0) | |
| after T1 | (0,1,1,0,0,0,0) | split: `pa`, `pb` |
| after T2b | (0,0,1,0,1,0,0) | shortcut fills `pd`, `pc` still 0 |
| after T4 | (0,0,0,0,1,1,0) | `pe` filled |
| after **T5** | (0,0,0,0,0,0,1) | join fires; **T3 has never fired** |

**Why 4 is minimal.** T5 needs tokens in `pd` and `pe` simultaneously.
- `pe` is produced only by T4 ← `pb` ← only by T1, so `T1 ≺ T4 ≺ T5`.
- To fire T5 *before* T3, the `pd` token cannot come from T3; the only other producer is T2b ← `pa` ← only by T1, so `T1 ≺ T2b ≺ T5`.

Thus three distinct firings `T1, T2b, T4` must precede T5, giving a lower bound of 4, which the sequence above attains. (Afterwards `pf` may feed T6 → `pc`, enabling T3, but that is strictly later.)
