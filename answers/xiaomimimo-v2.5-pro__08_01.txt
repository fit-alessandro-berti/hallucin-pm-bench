# Yes — T5 **can** fire before T3.

## Concrete firing sequence

$$T_1 \;\rightarrow\; T_2 \;\rightarrow\; T_5$$

## Step-by-step justification

| Step | Fire | Marking after firing |
|------|------|----------------------|
| 0 (initial) | — | p0(1), p1(0), p2(0), p3(0), p4(0), p5(0), p6(0) |
| 1 | **T1** | p0(0), **p1(1)**, **p2(1)**, p3(0), p4(0), p5(0), p6(0) |
| 2 | **T2** | p0(0), p1(0), p2(1), **p3(1)**, p4(0), p5(0), p6(0) |
| 3 | **T5** | p0(0), p1(0), p2(1), p3(0), p4(0), p5(0), **p6(1)** |

**Why T5 is enabled at step 3:**

- **Normal precondition (p3):** After T2 fires, p3 holds 1 token. ✓
- **Inhibitor precondition (p4):** p4 has **zero** tokens (nothing has touched it yet). ✓

**Crucially:** T5 consumes the token in p3, leaving p2 still holding its token. From this marking, only **T3** is left to fire (leading to p4 getting a token), but the point is already made — T5 has fired and T3 has not. ∎

> **Intuition:** T1 creates a race between two parallel branches. By firing T2 first (depositing a token in p3) and deliberately *not* firing T3 (leaving p4 empty), the inhibitor condition on T5 is satisfied, allowing it to fire.
