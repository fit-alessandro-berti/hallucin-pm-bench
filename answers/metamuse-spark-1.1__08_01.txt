Yes. $T_5$ requires:

* $M(p_3) \ge 1$
* $M(p_4) = 0$ by inhibitor

Both can be satisfied with $T_3$ not yet fired.

> $M_0 = [1,0,0,0,0,0,0]$ for $p_0,p_1,p_2,p_3,p_4,p_5,p_6$

* $M_0$: $T_1$ enabled. Fire $T_1$: $p_0 \to p_1+p_2$
$$M_1 = [0,1,1,0,0,0,0]$$

* $M_1$: $T_2$ and $T_3$ enabled. Fire $T_2$: $p_1 \to p_3$
$$M_2 = [0,0,1,1,0,0,0]$$

In $M_2$:

* $T_3$ is enabled because $M_2(p_2)=1$, not yet fired.
* $M_2(p_3)=1$ and $M_2(p_4)=0$, so the enabling condition for $T_5 : p_3 -|p_4 \to p_6$ is satisfied.
* $T_4$ is *not* enabled because $M_2(p_4)=0$.

So $T_5$ can fire:

* Fire $T_5$:
$$M_3 = [0,0,1,0,0,0,1]$$

Hence firing sequence

```
T1, T2, T5
```

and extended to `T1, T2, T5, T3` is a valid firing sequence from $M_0$ where $T_5$ occurs before $T_3$.

In $M_2$ the race is decided. If $T_3$ fires first, $p_4$ becomes $1$ and $T_5$ becomes permanently disabled; if $T_2$ fires first and $T_5$ is taken immediately, $T_5$ precedes $T_3$.
