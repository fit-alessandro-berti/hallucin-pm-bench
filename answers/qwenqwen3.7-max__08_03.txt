## Answer: **Yes**

It is possible for **T5** to fire before **T3**.

### Shortest firing sequence

```
T1 → T2b → T4 → T5
```

*(T1, T4, T2b, T5 is equally valid and equally short.)*

### Step-by-step marking trace

| Step | Fired | p0 | pa | pb | pc | pd | pe | pf | Enabled |
|------|-------|----|----|----|----|----|----|----|---------|
| 0 | — | 1 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | T1 |
| 1 | **T1** | 0 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | T2, T2b, T4 |
| 2 | **T2b** | 0 | 0 | 1 | 0 | **1** | 0 | 0 | T4 |
| 3 | **T4** | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 1 | **1** | 0 | **T5** |
| 4 | **T5** | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 1 | T6 |

### Why it works

T5 requires tokens in both **pd** and **pe**.

- **pe** can only be produced by **T4** (which consumes pb from T1).
- **pd** can be produced by either **T3** or the shortcut **T2b**.

By choosing **T2b** (the shortcut that skips pc entirely), pd is produced without ever enabling T3 (T3 requires a token in pc, which remains 0). T4 independently produces pe. Once both pd and pe hold a token, **T5 fires** — all without T3 ever having been enabled, let alone fired.

This sequence has length **4**, which is the theoretical minimum: T1 must fire first to produce pa and pb; then two independent transitions (T2b and T4) are needed to produce pd and pe; and finally T5 can fire.
