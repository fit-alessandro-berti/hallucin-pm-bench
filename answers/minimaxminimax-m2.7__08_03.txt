**Answer: Yes – T5 can fire before T3.**

---

### Why it is possible  

- **T5** needs one token in **pd** *and* one token in **pe**.  
- **T3** needs one token in **pc**.  

If we can produce the tokens for T5 while **pc** is still empty, T5 will be enabled and can fire before T3 ever becomes enabled.

The net supplies the needed tokens in the following way:

| Step | Transition fired | Consumed tokens | Produced tokens | Marking (only non‑zero places) |
|------|------------------|-----------------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| 0    | – (initial)      | –               | –               | p0 = 1 |
| 1    | **T1**           | p0              | pa, pb          | pa = 1, pb = 1 |
| 2    | **T2b**          | pa              | pd              | pb = 1, pd = 1 |
| 3    | **T4**           | pb              | pe              | pd = 1, pe = 1 |
| 4    | **T5**           | pd, pe          | pf              | pf = 1 |

After step 3 the marking is  

```
pd = 1 ,  pe = 1 ,  pc = 0
```

- **T5** is enabled (both its input places hold a token).  
- **T3** is *not* enabled because **pc** is still empty.

Thus firing **T5** at step 4 occurs **before** T3 has ever fired.

The sequence  

```
T1 , T2b , T4 , T5
```

is a reachable firing sequence in which T5 fires before T3.  
Because at least three “production” transitions (T1, T2b/T4) are required before the two inputs of T5 are available, **four** transitions is the minimal possible length – no shorter sequence can enable T5 before T3.

---

### Shortest firing sequence  

```
T1 → T2b → T4 → T5
```

(Any order of the independent transitions T2b and T4 after T1 gives the same length, e.g. T1 → T4 → T2b → T5.)

---

**Conclusion:** There exists a reachable marking (the one after firing T1, T2b, T4) in which T5 is enabled while T3 is not, so T5 can indeed fire before T3. The shortest way to achieve this is the four‑transition sequence shown above.
