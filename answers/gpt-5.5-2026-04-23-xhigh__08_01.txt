Yes.

One concrete firing sequence is:

\[
T1,\; T2,\; T5
\]

Markings:

1. Initial marking:

\[
M_0 = \{p0\}
\]

2. Fire \(T1: p0 \to p1+p2\):

\[
M_1 = \{p1,p2\}
\]

3. Fire \(T2: p1 \to p3\):

\[
M_2 = \{p2,p3\}
\]

At this point, \(T3\) has not fired yet. Also, \(p4\) has zero tokens, so the inhibitor condition for \(T5\) is satisfied. Since \(p3\) has a token, \(T5\) is enabled.

4. Fire \(T5: p3 \; -|p4 \to p6\):

\[
M_3 = \{p2,p6\}
\]

Thus, \(T5\) can indeed fire before \(T3\). The key point is that after \(T1,T2\), place \(p3\) is marked while \(p4\) is still empty, so the inhibitor arc from \(p4\) does not block \(T5\).
